White Metal Bearings

White metal bearings also referred to as babbitt bearings are made from babbitt alloy that is composed of Tin, Antimony and Copper and may also include lead, zinc and Bismuth. White metal bearings are generally used there is an application of high load our high RPM in such conditions white metal bearings performs up to the mark. Some properties of white metal that has made it such useful are low oil consumption, retention of lubricants, resistance to corrosion, good thermal conductivity etc. We would further discuss regarding composition, failure, repair and lubrication of white metal bearings.

Tin-lead and tin-copper alloys like Babbitt metal have ideal characteristics for plain bearings. Babbitt metal structure is made up of small hard crystals dispersed in a softer metal, making it a metal matrix composite. As bearing wears, softer metal erodes and creates path for lubricants to enter the structure between the hard spots, which are the main load bearing regions.

White Metal Bearing Composition

The main elements for composition of white metal or babbitt alloy are Tin, Copper and antimony besides this lead, Bismuth and Zinc also sometimes used. The main difference in different babbitt alloys is the percentage of the elements used. Tin may be used from 60 to 85 percentage whereas Copper and antimony ranges from 3 to 5 and 12 to 20 percentage respectively. Improper use of alloy in any bearings may lead to damage machine. also the use of lead based alloy instead of tin based alloy may cause damage to the machine. So it is very necessary to use the proper alloy for the specific application.

White Metal Bearing Lubrication

Lubrication of white metal bearings is same as other bearings. The white metal bearings should be lubricated regularly so as to prevent any damage to the lining of babbitt metal. However the bearings are self lubricated but the use of lubricant increases the life of the machine as well as the bearing. The leakage of the lubricant should be checked at regular intervals. Oe of the method of checking the lubricant leakage is checking the line where the white metal and steel shell are bonded. The sippage of oil from that area is very dangerous and may lead to severe damage.

Wiping Wear Marks

Fatigue - Mosaic Cracking

Fatigue Cracking - Resulting in Lining Loss

Fretting

Misalignment

Dirty Assembly

Cavitation Erosion

Discharge Cavitation Erosion

Corrosion

Tin Dioxide Corrosion

Sulphur Corrosion

Wire Wool Damage

Electrical discharge

Fretting Due To External Vibration

Overheating

Thermal Cycling Damage

Faulty Assembly

Incorrect Journal Grinding

Inadequate Oil Film Thickness

Inadequate lubrication

Bad Bonding of Lining to Shells
